For many beginners, Arabic verb conjugation seems like a monumental task. However, unlike the irregular verbs of English, Arabic verbs follow a highly logical and predictable pattern-based system. Once you understand the core concepts of root and pattern, conjugation becomes a skill you can master.

The Root and Pattern System

Most Arabic verbs are derived from a three-letter root (جذر) that carries a core meaning. For example, ف-ع-ل (f-'-l) means "to do." This root is then placed into different patterns (أوزان) to create different meanings and tenses.

We'll use the simplest pattern, Form I, for our examples.

Past Tense (الماضي - al-māḍī)

The past tense is formed by adding suffixes to the end of the verb root. These suffixes indicate the person, gender, and number of the subject.

Let's conjugate كَتَبَ (kataba - he wrote):

  • I wrote: كتبتُ (katabtu)
  • You (m.) wrote: كتبتَ (katabta)
  • You (f.) wrote: كتبتِ (katabti)
  • He wrote: كتبَ (kataba)
  • She wrote: كتبَتْ (katabat)
  • We wrote: كتبنا (katabnā)
  • They wrote: كتبوا (katabū)

Present Tense (المضارع - al-muḍāri')

The present tense is formed by adding both prefixes and suffixes. The prefixes are the most important part for identifying the subject.

Let's conjugate يكتب (yaktubu - he writes):

  • I write: أكتب (aktubu) - Prefix 'أ'
  • You (m.) write: تكتب (taktubu) - Prefix 'ت'
  • You (f.) write: تكتبين (taktubīna) - Prefix 'ت' and Suffix 'ين'
  • He writes: يكتب (yaktubu) - Prefix 'ي'
  • She writes: تكتب (taktubu) - Prefix 'ت' (same as "you m.")
  • We write: نكتب (naktubu) - Prefix 'ن'
  • They write: يكتبون (yaktubūna) - Prefix 'ي' and Suffix 'ون'

Tips for Mastery

Don't try to memorize every conjugation for every verb. Instead, focus on memorizing the patterns of suffixes and prefixes. Practice with common verbs like كتب (to write), قرأ (to read), and ذهب (to go). With consistent practice, the patterns will become automatic.
