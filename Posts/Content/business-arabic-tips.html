Business Arabic isn't a separate dialect but a register with specific vocabulary, formal structures, and cultural codes. "We started with the basics: proper titles. Understanding when to use 'سيدي' (Sir), 'حضرة' (Your Excellency), or 'أخي' (my brother) sets the tone for the entire relationship," Chen explains.

The Art of Business Greetings

First impressions in Arab business culture often involve elaborate greetings. "My coach drilled me on the 'كيف الحال؟' (How are you?) exchange," says Chen. "In business contexts, it's not a casual question. You're expected to ask about family, health, and business—often in that order—before discussing deals."

Negotiation language requires particular finesse. "Direct 'no' is rare. Instead, you hear 'إن شاء الله' (God willing) or 'سوف نرى' (we shall see). Learning to read between the lines was crucial." Positive phrases like 'فكرة ممتازة' (excellent idea) and 'تعاون مثمر' (fruitful cooperation) build rapport while maintaining professionalism.

"The contract was ready, but my client said 'بإذن الله' (with God's permission). My early training taught me this wasn't hesitation—it was cultural respect. I responded 'توكلنا على الله' (we trust in God), and we signed immediately."

Industry-Specific Terminology

Different sectors have specialized vocabularies. "In oil and gas, I needed terms like 'احتياطي نفطي' (oil reserves) and 'مصفاة' (refinery). In finance, 'تحويل عملة' (currency exchange) and 'سندات' (bonds). Arabfield Academy's industry modules prepared me for each sector."

Email etiquette proved equally important. "Opening with 'السلام عليكم ورحمة الله وبركاته' (peace be upon you) sets a respectful tone. Closing with 'تقبلوا فائق الاحترام' (please accept my highest respect) maintains professionalism. These formulas are non-negotiable in written communication."

Chen now conducts 80% of his Middle East business in Arabic. "It's not just about language proficiency—it's about building trust. When clients see you've invested in understanding their language and business culture, barriers disappear and opportunities multiply."
