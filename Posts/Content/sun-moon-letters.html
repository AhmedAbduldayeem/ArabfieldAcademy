One of the first pronunciation rules you'll encounter in Arabic is the curious case of the definite article 'al-' (ال). Sometimes the 'l' sound is pronounced clearly, and other times it disappears, causing the next letter to be doubled. This is all determined by whether the letter following 'al-' is a "sun letter" or a "moon letter."

What are Moon Letters (الحروف القمرية)?

Moon letters are the "easy" ones. When the definite article 'al-' comes before a moon letter, the 'l' (ل) is pronounced clearly and distinctly. The name comes from the word for "the moon," القمر (al-qamar), where you can clearly hear the 'l' sound.

There are 14 moon letters.

What are Sun Letters (الحروف الشمسية)?

Sun letters are the "tricky" ones. When 'al-' comes before a sun letter, the 'l' sound is NOT pronounced. Instead, it assimilates into the sun letter, which is then stressed or doubled (indicated by a shadda ّ symbol in writing). The name comes from the word for "the sun," الشمس (ash-shams), where the 'l' is silent and the 'sh' sound is doubled.

There are also 14 sun letters.

Why Does This Matter?

Mastering this rule is crucial for correct pronunciation and will make your spoken Arabic sound much more natural and fluent. It's one of the key indicators that separates a beginner from an intermediate speaker. While it might seem like a lot to memorize, you'll find that with listening and speaking practice, it will become second nature.
